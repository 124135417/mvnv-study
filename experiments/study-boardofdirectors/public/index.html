Inside America's Boardrooms

See how the biggest U.S. companies stack up in terms of board pay, independence and women directors. The Wall Street Journal has analyzed the boards of directors at S&P 500 companies.

Women

Women make about 20% of all directors at S&P 500 companies. Nearly three-quarters of boards have at least two women directorsâ€“up sharply from a decade ago, when only half of boards did. Still, the U.S. lags behind many European countries, including Norway, where more than one-third of directors are women.

Women make up half or more of the boards of just three large companies: Navient, Michael Kors and Tegna (formerly Gannett).

Eleven companies had all-male boards, including Discovery Communications, down from 58 in 2005.

Independence

At most companies, the vast majority of board members are deemed independent from the company and management under stock-exchange rules.

Three boards have only independent directors, as defined by data provider MSCI.

CEOs serve as board chairmen at just under half of S&P 500 companies, and former CEOs lead another 20%. Directors today are more likely to have had a hand in hiring the CEO: About half of all directors have held their seats longer than the company's CEO has been on the job, compared with just over a third in 2005.

Age

The median age of S&P 500 directors today is 63, up from 61 a decade ago. There are 71 directors who are at least 80 years old. Just 28 directors are under 40. Several of the oldest and youngest directors are company founders.

CBS's 14-member board is one of the oldest, where four directors are at least 80 and the youngest is 61.

Facebook has the youngest board member, Mark Zuckerberg, who is 31 years old. He is also the founder and CEO.

Tenure

Overall, average board tenure has lengthened slightly since 2005. However, newcomers are fairly common. About 15% of directors have held their seats for a year or less, while about one-quarter have been on the board for less than three years. The majority of directors have held their seats between three and 12 years.

Total System Services has the longest-tenured board. Half of its directors have served at least 23 years.

Darden Restaurants is among those with the most newcomers on its board after activist investors successfully pushed to replace the entire board in the fall of 2014.

Pay

Director compensation hasn't received the regulatory or investor attention that executive pay has in recent years. In part, that is because it usually is lower, and it varies less among companies. At half of the companies, median annual pay for directors is between $229,927 and $294,749. (Pay figures are for nonemployee directors.)

At Regeneron Pharmaceuticals most directors made at least $1.76 million in 2014, and at Celgene all but one director made more than $800,000.

At two companies, half or more of the board went unpaid: HCA Holdings, where eight directors stepped down early in the year, and at Amazon.com., where only a newly named director received a restricted stock award.

Berkshire Hathaway didn't pay any of its directors more than $6,700 in 2014.
